Step 0
Pilot Readiness Check — Complete Before Deploying Any Action
5 questions · 2 minutes · tells you where to start
▾

Before running a single workshop, validate that the basics are in place. A pilot that launches without sponsor alignment or process clarity will produce noise, not results.

Q1
Does the CCO actively sponsor this initiative — not just endorse it?
Q2
Is there a shared definition of "qualified lead" (MQL → SQL) that Sales and Marketing can both accept?
Q3
Have the 3 pilot markets committed one Sales and one Marketing person each for the duration?
Q4
Is IT available (even at limited capacity) to configure MS Dynamics for pilot workflows?
Q5
Is there agreement that the pilot will produce a visible "early win" result shared globally?
🧭
The real problem isn't the CRM — it's that Sales and Marketing don't share a definition of a lead.
Inconsistent lead management is a process and culture problem first, a technology problem second. Pilots that lead with IT configuration before resolving the handover definition rarely achieve lasting adoption. The sequence that works: align on process → agree the data model → configure lightly → train and embed. Simple and used beats sophisticated and ignored.

Conceptual Approach — How I Structure the Problem
Task 1: Problem framing, key principles, and the 3-horizon solution structure
📐
The rule: Lead harmonisation is not an IT rollout. It is a commercial alignment programme that uses technology as its backbone. Lead with process, follow with platform, embed with people.
🔄
On structure: The 3-phase plan is a safety net, not a schedule. Phase gates are go/no-go checkpoints — not bureaucracy. If conditions allow, phases compress. If a gate reveals a problem, it's better to catch it at week 4 than fix it at week 12. I'd rather kill a bad launch early than rescue a live one. Pragmatist first, process second.
1
Problem Framing · Root cause before solutions PL
Define the 3 layers of the problem
The challenge operates at three levels simultaneously. Layer 1 — Definition: "Qualified lead" means different things in every market, creating an invisible barrier between Marketing and Sales. Layer 2 — Behaviour: Sales bypasses CRM (email forwarding); Marketing generates leads with no handover SLA. Layer 3 — Visibility: Leadership cannot see pipeline health because data lives in inboxes, spreadsheets, and local tools. Solving Layer 3 without addressing Layers 1 and 2 first is unlikely to hold.
Success looks like: Every pilot market team can state — in identical terms — what makes a lead qualified and who owns it at each stage.
2
Key Principle · Design with, not for Both
Co-design the standard — don't impose it
The sceptical market ("global standard won't fit us") and the impatient Sales manager are signals of a participation deficit, not a logic problem. The fastest path to adoption is to involve local Sales and Marketing leads in designing the standard process — not presenting it to them. Run facilitated as-is → to-be workshops in each pilot market (not remote slide decks). The output is a jointly-owned process map that local teams believe is partly theirs. Co-ownership reduces resistance by an order of magnitude. Critically, co-design has a deadline: all markets must reach a working standard by end of week 3. If consensus fails, the CCO makes the tie-breaking call — participation is offered, consensus is preferred, but the decision is not deferred indefinitely. This is agreed with the CCO in week 1.
Constraint awareness: With only one Sales and one Marketing person per market, workshop facilitation must be time-efficient. Use structured templates, not blank canvases.
Veto risk: A politically powerful Market Head can stall co-design under the guise of "local complexity." The CCO tie-break authority must be communicated before workshops begin — not invoked as a surprise.
3
Key Principle · Minimum viable process first PL
Build the MVP lead process, not the perfect one
The pilot is not about building a world-class CRM. It is about proving that a simple, consistent lead process works better than the current inconsistency. The MVP has four elements: (1) agreed MQL/SQL definitions, (2) a lead entry point in MS Dynamics, (3) a handover trigger with a 48h SLA, (4) a basic dashboard showing leads by status. Everything else is phase 2. Tool-agnostic means: configure what's already licensed before requesting new tools.
Success looks like: All pilot markets use the same 4-stage lead pipeline in Dynamics within 8 weeks of kick-off, with daily active usage from both Sales and Marketing.
4
Key Principle · Adoption is the product Both
Make adoption visible and reward it
The brief asks for a pilot that makes "local organisations want more." Adoption metrics should be as visible as process metrics. Track CRM usage, lead entry compliance, and handover SLA adherence alongside conversion rates. Celebrate early wins — a market's first digital lead converted under the new process is worth sharing. Use the feedback loop to surface issues quickly and resolve them in short cycles. The influential Sales manager is an asset if engaged early.
Facilit8 principle: Change that sticks is change that people chose. Make the new way the easier way, not just the mandated way.
5
Scale design · Built-in from day one CCO
Design the pilot so scale is the obvious next step
Every artefact — process maps, CRM configuration, training materials, SLA templates — is documented as a reusable, market-agnostic asset. Pilot markets become internal advocates and peer coaches for the next wave. A retrospective at week 14 surfaces improvement areas before they compound. The CCO's live dashboard is the proof point that supports global scale approval.
Success looks like: At week 16, the CCO has a live view of lead status across all 3 markets and a clear plan to onboard the next wave of markets in the following quarter.
6
Selection criteria · Not all markets are equal PL
Choose pilot markets deliberately — four criteria
Criterion 1 — Willing Market Head: Active participation, not volunteered by a Cluster Head. Selection criteria are agreed with the CCO in week 1 — before Cluster Heads are consulted — giving the PL standing to question any nomination without it becoming a day-one political act.

Criterion 2 — Some existing CRM usage: Markets starting entirely from zero add configuration complexity that obscures process learnings. A partial Dynamics footprint is sufficient.

Criterion 3 — Manageable scale and clean conditions: Avoid markets that are large by revenue, customer base, or headcount — the 16-week window requires contained complexity. Also exclude markets with competing change programmes: ERP harmonisation, active acquisition integration, an existing transformation pilot, or significant leadership transition. A region with heavy acquisition activity and a parallel ERP rollout carries too many variables to produce clean learnings.

Criterion 4 — Balanced portfolio: Prefer 2 markets with straightforward conditions and 1 with a defined degree of challenge. Challenge is assessed against observable factors: a sceptical Market Head on record, below-average CRM usage relative to other candidates, or a history of low participation in previous global initiatives. The PL scores each candidate on a simple grid before presenting a shortlist to the CCO. If other criteria already introduce difficulty, all 3 markets can be straightforward.
Selection risk: Cluster Heads may nominate compliant markets rather than representative ones. The PL should validate each market against these four criteria before confirming the selection.

Workplan — Phases, Work Packages & Milestones
Task 2: Major work packages across 3 phases of the 16-week pilot
📐
Structure: 3 phases — Discover & Design (Wks 1–4), Build & Pilot (Wks 5–12), Embed & Scale-Prep (Wks 13–16). Each phase has clear entry criteria, deliverables, and a milestone gate.
🔍 Phase 1 — Discover & Design (Weeks 1–4)
Phase 1 Foundation
WP1.1 — As-Is Process Mapping (Wks 1–2): Facilitated workshops in each of the 3 pilot markets. Document current lead sources, qualification criteria, CRM usage, and handover points. Identify the 3 biggest improvement areas per market. Output: As-Is process map × 3 markets.

WP1.2 — Global Standard Design (Wks 2–3): Synthesise market findings. Co-design a standard 4-stage lead pipeline (New → MQL → SQL → Opportunity) with global Marketing and pilot market leads. Agree MQL/SQL definitions. Output: Standard Lead Process v1 + MQL/SQL definition document.

WP1.3 — CRM Data Model & IT Brief (Wks 3–4): Translate process to MS Dynamics field requirements. Write a lightweight IT configuration brief (not a full spec). Agree with IT on delivery timeline within budget envelope. Output: IT brief, Dynamics configuration scope, resource sign-off.

WP1.4 — Stakeholder Alignment (Wks 1–4, parallel): CCO briefing at kick-off. Market Head 1:1s. Cluster Head update. Change communication plan drafted.
Success looks like: By end of week 4, all pilot market Sales and Marketing leads have signed off the standard process and the IT brief is approved.
Phase gate: Moving into Phase 2 works best once all 3 markets have signed off the standard process and the IT brief is in place. This checkpoint is worth protecting — a week's patience here typically saves several weeks of rework later.
🔧 Phase 2 — Build & Pilot (Weeks 5–12)
Phase 2 Core Delivery
WP2.1 — CRM Configuration (Wks 5–7): IT configures MS Dynamics per brief. PL reviews with market representatives at week 6 checkpoint. UAT with 2 users per market. Output: Configured Dynamics pipeline live in UAT.

WP2.2 — Training Development (Wks 6–7): Build lightweight training pack (1-pager process guide + 30-min live session per market). Translate core materials into market languages where needed. Output: Training pack + facilitator guide.

WP2.3 — Go-Live Market A (Wk 8): First market goes live. PL on-site or high-touch remote for launch week. Daily check-in for 2 weeks. Collect feedback log.

WP2.4 — Go-Live Markets B & C (Wks 9–10): Staggered launch using lessons from Market A. Market A peer-advocates briefed. 48h SLA tracked from day 1.

WP2.5 — Dashboard & Reporting (Wks 8–12): Activate lead status dashboard for CCO visibility. Weekly pipeline report template live. Output: Live CCO dashboard.
Success looks like: By end of week 12, all 3 markets are logging leads in Dynamics, the 48h SLA is being tracked, and the CCO dashboard shows data from all markets.
IT dependency: Configuration must be complete by week 7. Any IT delay compresses training time — flag early and escalate to CCO if needed.
🚀 Phase 3 — Embed & Scale-Prep (Weeks 13–16)
Phase 3 Both
WP3.1 — Adoption Review & Improvement (Wks 13–14): Review CRM usage rates, SLA compliance, and qualitative feedback across all 3 markets. Run a lightweight retrospective. Identify top 3 improvement areas. Implement quick fixes within existing configuration (no new IT scope).

WP3.2 — Early Results Report (Wk 14–15): Compile pilot results: lead volume, conversion rate, SLA compliance, qualitative adoption scores. Frame as "proof of concept" narrative for CCO and board. Output: 1-page results summary + 5-slide CCO deck.

WP3.3 — Scale Playbook (Wk 15–16): Document all reusable assets: process maps, CRM configuration guide, training materials, SLA templates, onboarding checklist for new markets. Identify 5 candidate markets for next wave. Output: Scale playbook v1.

WP3.4 — Pilot Close & Handover (Wk 16): Formal pilot close meeting with CCO and cluster heads. Hand process ownership to designated Market Process Owners. Agree next-wave timeline.
Success looks like: At the pilot close, the CCO endorses the scale playbook and at least 2 of the 3 pilot markets have a nominated Process Owner who can support onboarding of new markets.

Sustainable Integration — HOPs, Performance & Ownership
Task 4: How the pilot embeds into ways of working through Harmonised Operating Procedures, performance management, and local ownership
🔗
Connection to the Impact Case: The 10 new capabilities defined in the Impact Case tab are the build list for Sustainable Integration. HOPs, the handover protocol, local process ownership, the feedback loop, and the scale playbook are not pilot outputs to be archived — they are the operating infrastructure that makes results hold after the PL exits. If a capability from the Impact Case is not embedded here, it will not survive the transition to business-as-usual.
📐
HOPs — Harmonised Operating Procedures is a term coined for this programme deliberately. "Harmonised" signals alignment without uniformity; "Operating Procedures" signals practical rhythm, not policy. The name itself carries the message: this is something teams work with daily, not a document filed away. 80% global standard — common pipeline, shared definitions, consistent handover protocol, unified reporting. 20% locally adapted — market-specific lead sources, language, and channel preferences. HOPs are a jointly-designed working agreement that local teams help shape and own.
What HOPs Cover — The Operating Standard
BothWk 3 designed · Wk 8 live
HOPs translate the agreed process into a day-to-day operating rhythm. Four areas:

1. Lead entry & qualification: How leads enter the pipeline (digital, event, referral, outbound), who qualifies them, and what the MQL threshold means in each market context.

2. Handover protocol: The trigger, owner, and 48h SLA for MQL→SQL transfer — with shared language for what happens when a lead is returned or doesn't progress.

3. CRM discipline: What gets logged, when, and by whom. The 20% local layer covers field names, language, and any market-specific stages — the 4-stage pipeline structure is shared.

4. Reporting rhythm: How pipeline data flows to Market Heads, Cluster Heads, and the CCO — weekly automated dashboard, monthly review.
Success looks like: A sales rep in Market A and a marketing manager in Market C can describe the handover process in equivalent terms — even if local lead generation differs.
Governing the 20%: The local adaptation layer is defined as a named list of permitted variations in the HOPs reference card — lead source labels, language, and channel routing. Anything outside that list is reviewed by the Process Owner and Global Marketing Lead before being adopted. The boundary is explicit; it does not expand case-by-case.
Performance Management — Cadence & Incentives
CCOPL
Process without performance management reverts to habit. New ways of working need to be visible in how teams are reviewed and recognised.

Review cadence: Market Heads reference the Dynamics dashboard in weekly 1:1s with Sales and Marketing leads. Cluster Heads receive a bi-weekly summary. The CCO holds a monthly pipeline review with lead conversion trend as a standing item.

Incentive alignment: The PL works with the CCO and HR to explore whether lead-quality metrics can feed into future team targets. The goal in the pilot is to open that conversation and document a recommendation — formal changes to targets are a next-wave activity.

Recognition: Early wins are shared publicly — a market's first fully-tracked digital lead conversion under the new process is featured in the CCO update. Peer visibility motivates at this stage more than policy.
Realistic expectation: Incentive changes take time and require HR alignment. The goal in the pilot is to begin the conversation and document the recommendation — formal changes to targets are a next-wave activity, not a week-16 deliverable.
Ownership Model — Process Owners & Community of Practice
BothLong-term
Ownership transfers before the PL exits — not after. Each pilot market designates a Process Owner at go-live (week 8 at latest) — ideally the person who co-designed the process. Their role: monthly data review, flag exceptions, propose local adaptations within the HOPs 20% flexibility, and represent their market in the Community of Practice.

The Lead Process Community of Practice meets monthly for 30 minutes. The Global Marketing Lead is agreed as chair in week 1 as part of their process co-owner brief — framed as a natural extension of co-designing the standard, not an additional obligation assigned later. The PL contributes to the first two sessions, then steps back. Templated agenda: 10 min pipeline trends / 10 min process issue and resolution / 10 min new market updates. New markets join as observers before their own go-live — arriving with peer context rather than headquarters instructions.
Success looks like: Every pilot market has a named Process Owner by week 8, and the Community of Practice runs its third session without PL involvement — with at least one non-pilot market joining as an observer ahead of their own onboarding.

Stakeholder Engagement — Getting People on Board
Task 3: How I involve, convince, and govern the stakeholder map
🤝
Engagement principle: Different stakeholders need different things. The CCO needs confidence. Market Heads need respect for their context. Sales and Marketing reps need to feel heard — and to see the win quickly.
👔
CCO — Chief Commercial Officer
Needs global visibility without getting into the weeds
The CCO is the primary sponsor and the scale decision-maker. They care about pipeline visibility, conversion improvement, and a credible path from pilot to global. Frame everything in commercial outcomes — not CRM features. Give them a live dashboard they can show the board.
Invitation script
"[CCO name], I want to confirm the pilot design with you before week 2 starts. I'll show you the 3 markets, the timeline, and the one dashboard metric we'll use to prove success. 20 minutes. No prep needed."
For relationship-led cultures, add: "I've heard good things about your work with the clusters — I'd love your read on how Market A's head typically responds to global initiatives."
🌍
Market Head — Sceptical Market
"Global standard won't fit us" — fears loss of local autonomy
This person has seen global initiatives fail before because they ignored local context. They are not wrong to be cautious — they need to see that their voice will shape the standard, not just receive it. Co-design is the unlock. Give them a seat at the table in week 3's global standard workshop.
Invitation script
"[Name], your market's experience is exactly what we need to get the standard right. I'd like your team to help design the process — not just test it. Can we set up a 90-minute workshop in week 2?"
For relationship-led cultures: "I've been told your market has the most sophisticated local setup — I want to make sure we learn from that before we build anything."
💼
Influential Sales Manager
Impatient — wants results, not process discussions
This person's informal authority can make or break adoption in their market. They will test the new process by whether it makes their job easier, not by whether it's strategically aligned. Show them a quick win — a lead that converts faster under the new process. Make them the go-live champion.
Invitation script
"[Name], I know you're under pressure to deliver. The pilot is designed to reduce the friction between you and Marketing — and give you leads you can actually close. I need 2 hours from you in week 2. After that, the process works for you."
For relationship-led cultures: "I'd rather have your honest read on what's broken than guess from the outside — this is your chance to fix it on your terms."
💻
IT Lead
Overloaded — every project thinks it's urgent
IT's limited capacity is the most fragile dependency in the workplan. They need a locked, minimal scope brief — not an open conversation about possibilities. Treat them as a delivery partner, not a service desk. Protect their time from stakeholder requests arriving outside the PL's channel.
Invitation script
